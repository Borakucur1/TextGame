Text Me When You're Home

You hear the car before you fully see it.

Slow tires against wet pavement. Passenger window lowering halfway.

“You good?” the driver asks.

You stop walking.

“I can drive you. It's on my way.”

Normally you'd never even consider it. But tonight your feet hurt, the train situation is a mess, and the alcohol keeps trying to convince you that instinct is the same thing as paranoia.

The guy seems normal enough.

Clean hoodie. Calm voice. Music low.

You hesitate for half a second, then choose the option that feels easiest: letting someone else decide for you.

You get in.

The doors lock automatically.

Neither of you says much for the first two blocks.

The city outside changes slowly. Fewer people. Fewer storefronts. Longer stretches of dark between streetlights.

You tell yourself it's fine.

You tell yourself you're being dramatic.

Your phone lights up in your lap.

1% battery.

And suddenly it hits you all at once:

You never texted anyone.

No shared location. No last message. No one watching your dot move across the map.

If something happened right now, nobody would even know where to start looking.

For the first time all night, you feel completely sober.
