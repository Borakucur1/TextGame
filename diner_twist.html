Text Me When You're Home

You step into the diner like your body made the decision before your brain could.

Warm air hits your face immediately. Coffee. Grease. Fluorescent lights bright enough to feel medicinal.

A worker behind the counter glances up. “You okay?”

You nod too fast.

Someone slides you a cup of water without asking, like they've seen this exact version of the night happen hundreds of times before.

The city outside suddenly feels very far away through the fogged-up windows.

Your phone buzzes violently against the counter.

“Where are you?”

You send a blurry picture of the diner menu and your location.

Three dots appear instantly.

“Stay inside. Don't move. I'm ordering you an Uber right now.”

Two minutes later: a screenshot. Car make. Plate number. Driver name. Pickup spot directly outside the window.

“Only get in if EVERYTHING matches.”

“Text me when you're inside the car.”

“Then text me when you're home.”

The car finally pulls up.

You check the plate like it's the most important test you've ever taken.

It matches.

Inside the car, the heat is too high and the radio is playing something soft and forgettable.

For the first time all night, your shoulders unclench.

The streets blur past the window. Neon signs. Empty intersections. Steam rising from somewhere underground.

And then eventually:

Your building.

Your keys.

Your door.

Safe.

At your door, you finally send the sacred text:

home.

Your friend replies instantly:

“THANK GOD.”

“No more drinking for you.”

You actually laugh this time.
